I thought I'd try a writing exercise. A while back I got a couple sets of "Rory's Story Cubes," which are dice with little pictures on them meant to prompt some kind of a story. I picked three at random and gave them a roll. Below is the story I came up with. I didn't spent too long polishing it; the idea was to use this to exercise my "writing muscle," if you will. Anyway, I hope you enjoy!

==========

Lucas stared dully down at the rusty bucket sitting in the sand in front of him. The crabs inside scuttled around, picking fights with one another. Occasionally one would attempt to crawl free before being pulled back down by one of its fellow prisoners. The young tiger growled faintly. They were just like everyone else he’d ever known. Everyone always pulled each other down, even when you were so close climbing out of whatever hole you were in. His throat tightened as he remembered what had happened only a few days ago.

His brother had found him an old wooden crutch. It was too tall for Lucas, scratched, dirty and missing the cushion on top. But after only a day of work, some scraps of fabric, and some twine, it was sturdy enough to support his weight. Lucas had eagerly taken it out the very next day. Before the day was over, it was in pieces. The same kids who’d teased him for as long as he could remember had taken his crutch and shattered it against the ground. It wasn’t fair! They mocked him for not being able to walk right, and then took away the one thing that might actually help.

Lucas felt hot tears spill from his eyes as he continued to glare down at the bucket of crabs. One of them had actually made it to the rim somehow. It flailed its legs about, trying to heft the rest of its body over the edge where it would be able to topple down to the sand and be free. Lucas reached over and flicked the crab back into the bucket where its merciless peers quickly piled over the top of it to take their own turn at trying to escape. “That’s what you get,” Lucas told the crab, bitterly.

He was startled out of his brooding by the sudden appearance of four more large crabs which fell from above into the bucket. He glanced up and saw his brother, Paulo. He stood there, wet from his swim, looking down at Lucas, his tail twitching occasionally behind him. He didn’t look angry or ashamed. Just pensive. Lucas looked away, sullenly. “C’mon,” Paulo said, turning around and kneeling, his back towards his younger brother. Lucas shuffled awkwardly towards him, walking on his front paws and dragging his bent legs over the sand. Then he wrapped his arms around Paulo’s shoulders and clasped them tightly. With a grunt, the older tiger stood up, lifting Lucas off the ground with him. Lucas’ tail barely touched the ground. He was getting taller. And heavier. Paulo never complained. But Lucas knew that eventually, he would be too old to be carried like this.

With Lucas on his back and the bucket of crabs held in one paw, Paulo trudged slowly along the beach towards the village where he would sell the crabs he had caught, except for one or two that they would cook and eat themselves later that evening.

“We got some big ones today. Thanks for keeping an eye on them,” Paulo said as he walked. Lucas just grunted. *Paulo* had caught them with his bare paws. Lucas had just sat there on the beach feeling miserable. “We keep this up and in a few weeks, we might be able to afford a real crab trap. We can catch more and earn more.” Lucas still said nothing, so they continued on in silence until they reached the docks where several other beasts hawked their wares and catches of the day. Paulo deposited Lucas on a shady spot near the docks. “Stay here while I see what we can get for these,” he said.

“I can’t do anything else,” Lucas said moodily.

Paulo smiled sympathetically, bent down, and licked Lucas behind the ear. Just like their father had always done. “I won’t be long,” he said, and then turned and left Lucas alone with his thoughts.

Lucas stared out to sea, watching wave after wave roll into the beach. He found himself wishing he were a dolphin or a sea turtle, buoyed up by the water instead of weighed down by his own useless legs. He heard the cry of a gull and looked up to see one of the birds hovering on the warm tropical air current. Now he wished he were a bird, so light and unencumbered that he practically floated away. The gull he was watching suddenly dropped out of the sky and landed on the sandy beach below where it was quickly joined by two others. They had found a crab on the beach which had chosen the wrong moment to come out from hiding. Though it tried to fend them off with its claws, the hungry birds continued to pick and peck at it. Lucas turned away in disgust. He wasn’t a turtle or a dolphin or a bird. He was a crab. Good for nothing but to drag his body across the sand and feebly attempt to ward off those that picked on him and preyed on his own helplessness.

As he stared at the ground, his thoughts were interrupted by an object entering his vision. Something flat and brightly colored slid into view, coming to rest after softly bumping into his knee. Lucas cautiously placed a paw on it and lifted it up. It was a skateboard. The top was painted sky blue with a bright yellow streak of lightning down the middle. The wheels on the bottom were in good condition and turned smoothly. He glanced up to see where it had come from. Paulo stood there in front of him, grinning broadly. “Well, what do you think?” he asked.

Confused, Lucas looked down at the board again. “It’s… cool,” he said.

“Get on,” Paulo said, stooping down. Lucas allowed him to gently lift him up onto the board, still unsure what was going on. He sat on it, feeling it wobble slightly until he found his balance. He looked up at his brother who nodded encouragingly. “Go ahead! See how well you can move around.” Lucas placed his paws on the ground and pushed, propelling himself forward. He moved with surprising ease; much easier than dragging himself along the ground or even hobbling on a crutch.

After going some distance, Lucas leaned back on the board causing the front wheels to lift up. Catching himself with his back paw, he shifted his weight to turn the board around until he was facing Paulo again and then pushed himself back towards him. He couldn’t prevent a smile from appearing on his face. “That’s… fun,” he admitted.

Paulo nodded as if satisfied. “I got you these too,” he said, tossing him a pair of gloves with thick, rough material on the palms. “To help your paws not to get too sore from pushing against the ground all day.”

Lucas suddenly looked back at Paulo in shock. “Wait, you… you got this for me? This… is mine…?” he asked, placing a reverent paw on top of the board.

Paulo nodded again. “Someone was selling it second-hand and I thought it might help,” he said casually. “It’s not a perfect solution. It won’t work on the beach or where it’s too steep. But on the pier, the roads, or any place where the ground is hard it should do just fine.”

“But… I thought you wanted to get a crab trap. You had a plan to help us earn more so you wouldn’t have to work so hard.”

Paulo shrugged. “It’s fine. It’ll just take a few more months to earn back what these cost. A year, tops. I just wanted you to be happy in the meantime. That’s worth a hundred crab traps,” he said with a wink.

“Thank you,” Lucas said, and he felt tears welling up. But this time they were tears of happiness and gratitude. Paulo bent down and licked him behind the ear again.

Not everybody was a crab like Lucas had thought. There were still people in the world like his brother who tried to uplift rather than tear down. And that day Lucas decided he wouldn’t be a crab either.
